Get your hands dirty. Go plant something.

Dig a hole outside, feel the soil. It's colder than the air temperature. Is the soil dense? Is the soil loose? Wet? Dry? Rocky? Sandy? Already, you've learned something about your little patch of dirt.

Keep reading, and learn how to use that knowledge

Certain plants and microorganisms thrive in certain kinds of soil or climates. These particular locations have names. They are called Planting zones or Plant Hardiness Zones.

Click the link below to see a detailed map of Planting Zones in the United States

I happen to be in central eastern Missouri at the moment. We are Zone: 6a-6b

From here, you will assess the amount of water the site gets and how your particular soil handles water. Is it in a depression? On a slope?
Some plants like water logged roots, while others prefer an occassional drizzle.

My back yard is mostly sloped, meaning anything I plant there, needs to able to deal with drought, as the rain water will quickly flow down the hill before soaking into the soil.

So now we know we have clay soil, on a slope, receiving little water.
How much sun does it get?

My backyard slope faces north. It is partially shaded in the evening by a humungous sycamore. But gets blasted by sun in the morning and afternoon.

Luckily for us, most seed packages and plant tags have the plant's watering and sun needs printed directly on to it for our edification.
With our brain full of information, and hopefully, a few notes taken. We know we are looking for something that can thrive in dry, clay soil, with a blast of sun in the afternoon.
